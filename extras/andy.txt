# Andy / 2025-09-04

Andreas Nedbal <andy@pixelde.su>

- Built a site Retrospring about 11 years ago (went public in Dec 2014)
- Beta-tested privately and then soft launched
- Was a social QA website like Formspring
- Initially wasn't open source - became open source mid-2015
- Also had temporary shutdown then (personal politics)
  - The final shutdown was the third, and only fully happened four days ago
- Let site run without support from 2017, got remotivated, started working on it again in 2020
- Competitor had downtime in 2020, their site exploded then (gained 30K users in a month)
- The site became well-known without any marketing
- That continued until last year (2024)
- At which point they decided social QA wasn't really a useful thing
- The maintainers all had full-time jobs
- And people on social networks aren't all that nice
- Even with donations, the cost outweighed the benefit
  - They wouldn't want to do this full-time
- There were some internal clashes
- So they decided in August 2024 to shut down on March 1, 2025
- They published blog post start of Aug 2024 saying the site would shut down
  - Offered data export until Sept 1, 2025
  - Then contacted by archive team
  - Changed website into read-only instead of shutting it down
  - Ran read-only for six months w/ data export
- Had seen importance w/ other platforms of giving users time to export data and build alternatives
  - Takes time
- This wasn't a forced shutdown
  - Just two people isn't enough to keep it going
  - Negligence and optimism would eventually cause trouble
  - And wanted to ensure good memories in minds of users
  - Could keep it running, but w/ 200K users, not caring is not an option
- Everything stays open source
- Other instances have started up, but most have also since shut down
  - Social media is a very aggressive environment
  - Most people don't hold out for long
- Just Andy involved in decision to shut down
  - Other person who had been involved wasn't in the loop (by their choice)
- Believes it went as well as it could have
  - Six months between each step for the users to make plans
  - Used the system's announcement system
  - Looked at analytics on announcement post: had 80K views, so at least 50% of active user base
- Was surprised that no one has come back since to complain
  - Was expecting some hostility (open source, social media, users…)
  - At some point users start demanding things (consumers rather than active contributors)
  - Most people really were just sad: frustration turned into sadness rather than anger
- https://blog.retrospring.net/retrospring-is-shutting-down-on-1st-march-2025
- Q: where did you learn how to do this?
  - Negative examples: sites that just go down without warning
  - Positive example: the Mastodon Server Covenant for public instances https://joinmastodon.org/covenant
    - "Give at least a month's notice of shutdown"
    - Ample time for users to migrate
  - 15 years of personal experience with other services and how quickly they can disappear
- Q: is this changing what you do now?
  - Not currently running any projects that are this large
  - Had maintainers come and go over ten years of being open source
  - Lots of documentation, reviewed pull requests carefully
  - Never more than 2-3 people working on website at a time
  - If he was going to build another social media site, he would put proper governance structure in place
    - More people with decision-making power
