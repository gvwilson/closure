# Lars

Lars Wirzenius <liw@liw.fi>

- Brief biography
  - Started CS in 1988 hoping to be a prof
  - In 1991 helped start Linux and realized he wasn't interested in academia: wanted to build things
  - Familiar with NWIT
- First involvement with open source
  - Got access to BB and read GNU Manifesto in 1986
  - Made him want to save the world by writing software
  - Got access to FTP servers in '88 and found lots of interesting software to look at
- Origins of the project
  - Father introduced him to computers in 1984 on a home computer
  - "This floppy is where you put your files, this other floppy is where you put your backups"
  - Forty years later has never had a catastrophic data loss (has never really needed to do a restore)
  - Realized in early 2000s that floppies were too small for data he had, and so were CDs and DVDs
  - Realized in 2004 that hard drives were so cheap he could just make a backup there
  - Started looking for software to do that, and that takes advantage of the fact that he has a hard drive not a tape drive
  - Didn't find anything he liked (esp. not anything that would do encrypted remote snapshot backups)
  - Started a company in 2006 w/ a friend to build a business around Obnam
  - First version written in Python nad had a few hundred users
- Publicizing it?
  - Talked incessantly about it (blog, IRC, wherever)
  - Was happy with the number of users he had
  - Didn't care about being the world's most popular backup program
  - Problem was that he wrote bad code: fundamentally hard to make changes
  - Was too easy to make a change that broke something somewhere else, and no test suite is perfect
  - And it was (very) slow - slow enough to bother Lars as well as his users
- How it ended
  - Unhappy from about 2015 onward: a passion project had become a chore (felt obliged to continue supporting users)
  - No outside pressure telling him to stop
  - Decided in 2017 that backups were no longer interesting to him
  - Made a blog post saying that he'd stop maintaining it at the end of the year
  - A few months in which people were strongly encouraged to switch
  - Nothing bad happened in that time
  - People continued to use it: "It has been zero years since I got a bug report"
    - Someone needed to restore all his data: was very apologetic
    - Have been a few other requests over the decade: friendly, but need help, so he does what he can
- Who else involved in decision?
  - Just Lars: practically speaking a solo project
  - Some contributions from others but he was the only maintainer
- Shutdown mechanics
  - As far as he knows, nobody missed the announcement
- Lessons learned
  - Can't think of anything he would do differently
  - Might tell his younger self to shut it down earlier
  - Would also tell himself that he wouldn't really be done with backups
  - Has learned from his mistakes: latest is going in a different direction
- How do you feel about all of this now?
  - Feels good: it was the right thing to do
  - Could either hand it over or shut it down and there was nobody who was involved enough to take over
  - Could have spent a month searching for someone, but there were at least two spinoff projects that are still going on
  - "Obnam was so bad that people wanted to do better"
- Final comments?
  - All the interactions he had with other people were slightly irritating but friendly
  - People understood he didn't have time to work on this (esp. no same-day turnaround)
  - One user spent a year debugging why one client backup didn't work properly
  - Doesn't really know what he did right there

## Another story

- Wabid https://kannel.org/
- Startup founded in the late 1990s
- Money started drying up in 2000
- Immediate bankruptcy: developers just walked away
- The VCs did not treat them well when the money dried up
- Wasn't able to do anything to transition funded project into community project
- But people who were using it themselves started doing so
- Warm start by users, but no follow-through support from original developers
- It was like the original developers were cursed: nobody would talk to them
- Did right:
  - Very open project, transparent about internal communication
  - All worked in the same office, but kept everything on the public mailing list to help other people see what was done why
  - Architecture was simple
  - Telecoms protocols were complex and badly documented, but well enough for other people to pick up
- Could have done better:
  - Could have given the community more warning
    - But it's a very stressful time when a company is going bankrupt
    - And may not be allowed to tell people
  - Could have brought in more community members for maintenance earlier
    - Development and release management
- Doesn't know how the inheritors self-organized
  - Mailing list might still be publicly archived
